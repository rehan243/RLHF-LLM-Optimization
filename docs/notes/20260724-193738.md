## Preference Optimization Tradeoffs
When fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) with preference optimization techniques, I've found that the choice between PPO and DPO significantly impacts the model's behavior. **Reward modeling** is a crucial step in both methods, as it directly influences the model's learned preferences. PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) requires a separate reward model, which can be challenging to train and may introduce additional complexity. In contrast, DPO (Direct Preference Optimization) eliminates the need for a separate reward model, simplifying the process. However, DPO can be more sensitive to the KL (Kullback-Leibler) divergence control, which prevents the model from deviating too far from its original behavior. I've observed that setting the KL coefficient too low (e.g., 0.05) can lead to over-optimization, causing the model to produce nonsensical outputs. By increasing the KL coefficient to 0.2, I was able to maintain a better balance between preference optimization and output coherence.
